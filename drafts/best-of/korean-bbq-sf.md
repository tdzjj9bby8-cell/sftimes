## Editor's intro (draft)

> Korean BBQ is a category every SF list gets wrong. They either count anything with a brick of bulgogi as "Korean BBQ" or they grade on banchan count and call it a day. This list is the spots where the grill is actually the point, the meat is sliced like somebody cared, and the banchan is made that morning, not poured out of a Cambro. Refreshed quarterly. Editor below, no affiliate links, no chain restaurants, no Yelp ratings averaged together. If a place stops being good, it comes off.

## The list (strict tabletop-grill scope, 7 entries)

### 1. Han Il Kwan

- **Neighborhood:** Outer Richmond
- **Price:** $$$
- **What it is:** House banchan spread
- **Photo:** placeholder (`best-of/korean-bbq-sf/01-han-il-kwan.jpg`)

**Blurb (draft):**
For my money, the most complete Korean BBQ experience in the city. Twelve banchan land before you have decided what to order. The meat arrives sliced the way it should be, the spicy pork is a meal on its own, and the room has been calmly working at this since long before the trend cycle caught up. Closed Tuesday and Wednesday like a place that has earned the right to close two days a week.

**The pick (draft):**
When you bring out-of-town friends and want them to leave understanding why people actually live in the Richmond.

---

### 2. San Ho Won

- **Neighborhood:** Mission
- **Price:** $$$$
- **What it is:** Lychee charcoal galbi, Michelin Guide
- **Photo:** placeholder (`best-of/korean-bbq-sf/02-san-ho-won.jpg`)

**Blurb (draft):**
Corey Lee dropped a Benu pedigree on a Mission corner, lit lychee wood, and let the meat carry it. The prime double-cut galbi is the meal. Banchan is engineered, not piled on. You book 29 days out at 10 a.m. or you do not eat. It is not the cheapest night out in the city, but it is the most considered.

**The pick (draft):**
When you want Korean BBQ to feel like a tasting menu and you have a reservation alarm set.

---

### 3. Brothers Restaurant

- **Neighborhood:** Inner Richmond
- **Price:** $$
- **What it is:** Mesquite charcoal, three generations, since 1987
- **Photo:** placeholder (`best-of/korean-bbq-sf/03-brothers.jpg`)

**Blurb (draft):**
4128 Geary. Open since 1987, same family, three generations on the floor. The grills run on actual mesquite charcoal, not gas with a wood-chip-flavor. The dwen-jang in the soup is six months old when it hits your bowl. Banchan changes by the day because they make it that morning. No AYCE gimmick. Order the kal bi and pay attention.

**The pick (draft):**
When you want to eat where the family who built it is still in the kitchen.

---

### 4. Wooden Charcoal Korean Village BBQ House

- **Neighborhood:** Inner Richmond
- **Price:** $$
- **What it is:** Old-school charcoal, lunch-combo move
- **Photo:** placeholder (`best-of/korean-bbq-sf/04-wooden-charcoal.jpg`)

**Blurb (draft):**
One block east on Geary at 4611. The lunch combo with rice, soup, and seven banchan is the play if you cannot do a sit-down dinner with three friends. Soybean sprouts, two daikon kimchis, the pickled cucumber that does not show up at the newer places. The room is unfashionable. That is the point.

**The pick (draft):**
When you want a real charcoal grill and a $20 lunch instead of a $90 dinner.

---

### 5. Zajang Grill

- **Neighborhood:** Inner Richmond
- **Price:** $$
- **What it is:** AYCE done right
- **Photo:** placeholder (`best-of/korean-bbq-sf/05-zajang.jpg`)

**Blurb (draft):**
852 Clement. The AYCE everyone resists until they have done it once and admitted it is fine. $40.99 a head, 90 minutes, bulgogi that has actually marinated, miso pork belly that does not taste like it came out of a freezer bag, free ice cream on the way out. The murals are loud. The food is not.

**The pick (draft):**
When a group of six wants to spend $50 each and not have to think.

---

### 6. YakiniQ

- **Neighborhood:** Japantown
- **Price:** $$
- **What it is:** AYCE with a premium tier
- **Photo:** placeholder (`best-of/korean-bbq-sf/06-yakiniq.jpg`)

**Blurb (draft):**
Upstairs at 1640 Post. The premium AYCE gets you galbi, bulgogi, beef tongue, pork belly, pig intestine, and spicy chicken in a two-hour window. The basic tier is fine. The premium is the only one worth ordering. Service is fast, the hoods work, the soju is cheap.

**The pick (draft):**
When you want AYCE without the Clement Street line and you are OK with stairs and Japantown parking.

---

### 7. Um.ma

- **Neighborhood:** Inner Sunset
- **Price:** $$$
- **What it is:** Modern Korean with a grill patio
- **Photo:** placeholder (`best-of/korean-bbq-sf/07-umma.jpg`)

**Blurb (draft):**
1220 9th Ave. A hybrid, kept on the list because the back patio with tabletop grills is real. Order kalbi, bulgogi, and pork belly by the pound, and let one person who refuses to grill order the Korean fried chicken from the dining room menu. Skip if you want strict BBQ. Go if you want one room that covers a mixed group.

**The pick (draft):**
When half your table wants stew and the other half wants meat.
